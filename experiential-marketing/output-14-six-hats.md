# Fisher XP — Six Thinking Hats Analysis

Comprehensive stress test of the full proposition: market validation, positioning, service architecture, messaging, and founder capability. Adapted from Brand Schema Prompt 9a.

---

## White Hat (Facts): What Is Actually Validated vs. Assumed?

### Validated (Evidence Exists)

- The global experiential market is approximately $55.5bn (2026, Business Research Insights)
- The UK experiential market is approximately £2.1bn (Cognitive Market Research)
- UK events budgets are growing at +16.6% net balance (IPA Bellwether Q1 2025)
- 70% of event organisers report difficulty proving ROI (Bizzabo 2025)
- 48% of ISBA members do not believe they get value from agencies (ISBA 2025)
- Six overlapping regulatory frameworks create a genuine compliance surface for experiential
- No experiential agency has published a combined compliance readiness framework
- Both founders have verifiable experience at American Express, Aviva, and Alter Domus

### Assumed (Not Yet Tested)

- **The 25% disenchanted spend figure.** Triangulated from proxies. The Checkpoint flagged this as the weakest number in the entire evidence pack.
- **Tier 1 to Tier 2 conversion rate of 30-40%.** Estimated. No experiential-specific conversion data exists for a diagnostic-to-activation pipeline.
- **£250K-£400K Year 1 revenue target.** Capacity analysis suggests £180K-£280K is more realistic.
- **That compliance-integrated experiential commands a price premium.** The positioning stress test identified a failure scenario where compliance has zero price premium.
- **That financial services CMOs will hire a two-person company.** The single most dangerous untested assumption.
- **That the co-founder dynamic will survive financial stress.** Untested under real conditions.
- **Production partner margins of 35-45% on activations.** Depends on specific partner rate cards not yet negotiated.
- **10-day attribution reporting.** The measurement framework does not yet exist as a working system.

### Critical Gap

The ratio of assumed to validated is approximately 60:40. For a business about to launch, this is normal — but it means the first 6 months are a validation exercise, not an execution exercise. The founders must treat every early engagement as a test of assumptions, not confirmation of strategy.

## Red Hat (Intuition/Feeling): Gut Reaction

**Overall impression:** This reads like a well-researched business plan, not a well-tested business. The volume of strategic documentation is impressive — and that is the problem. There are now over 15 documents of analysis, positioning, messaging, and frameworks. Zero client engagements. Zero revenue. Zero proof that any of this resonates with a real buyer.

**The planning-to-action ratio is dangerously high.** Every additional document of analysis creates a feeling of progress without actually being progress. The founders risk falling into the same trap the Six Hats analysis at Brand Schema identified: "Is the volume of strategic documentation functioning as a substitute for selling?"

**The co-founder addition is genuinely strengthening.** Julian's brand governance and financial services experience fills the most important credibility gap the Red Team identified. The combination of Justin's experiential delivery and Julian's strategic rigour is more convincing than either alone. But the two founders have not yet been tested under the specific pressure of jointly running a startup with no revenue.

**The verbal identity is strong.** The voice — precise, grounded, direct, accountable — feels authentic to the founders' backgrounds. It does not sound like agency marketing. This is an asset, but only if the founders actually use it consistently. Many founders develop a voice guide and then revert to generic language under time pressure.

**The positioning is intellectually sound but emotionally flat.** "The Regulated Experience House" is clear and defensible. It is not exciting. It does not make a CMO lean forward. The messaging framework partially addresses this ("creative that survives"), but the dominant impression is still: compliance consultancy that happens to do events, rather than: creative experiential firm that happens to be compliance-proof.

## Black Hat (Critical Judgment): Five Failure Modes

### Failure Mode 1: The Credibility Deadlock
**Probability: 40%**

Buyers want case studies. Fisher XP has none. Buyers want references. Fisher XP can offer personal references from Amex and Aviva but not Fisher XP client references. The first client must take a risk on an unproven company — and in regulated financial services, risk-taking on suppliers is culturally discouraged.

**What would need to be true for this to fail:** No personal relationship strong enough to bypass formal procurement in the first 5 months. If both founders' networks fail to produce a founding client willing to take a chance, the business stalls before it starts.

### Failure Mode 2: Cash Exhaustion Before Revenue
**Probability: 35%**

The unit economics show a maximum cash gap of ~£119K at Month 9. If the founders do not have £60K-£80K in starting capital, or if the first major payment is delayed by even one month, the business runs out of money. Experiential has particularly punishing cash flow dynamics — subcontractors must be paid before clients pay you.

**What would need to be true for this to fail:** Starting capital below £50K combined, AND first engagement delayed beyond Month 5, AND payment terms of 60+ days.

### Failure Mode 3: Positioning Rejected by Market
**Probability: 25%**

The market may not segment the way the research suggests. Buyers may not distinguish between "compliance-integrated experiential" and "experiential agency that says they do compliance." If every agency starts claiming compliance capability within 12 months (which the positioning stress test predicts), Fisher XP's differentiation evaporates.

**What would need to be true for this to fail:** Buyers treat compliance as a checkbox rather than a value driver, AND a major agency launches a competing compliance practice within 12 months of Fisher XP's launch.

### Failure Mode 4: Production Failure on First Engagement
**Probability: 20%**

Fisher XP does not own production capability. The first activation depends entirely on subcontracted production partners. If a production partner underdelivers — late, over budget, quality issues — Fisher XP bears the reputational damage. One production failure on the first or second engagement could be unrecoverable for a new brand.

**What would need to be true for this to fail:** Production partner selected on cost rather than reliability, OR insufficient quality control process, OR scope poorly defined in the subcontractor agreement.

### Failure Mode 5: Co-Founder Misalignment Under Stress
**Probability: 20%**

Justin and Julian have complementary skills and shared history (both worked at Amex, both worked with Alter Domus). But they have not run a business together. Financial stress, disagreements about pricing, client prioritisation, and work allocation can fracture partnerships — especially when both founders are forgoing income.

**What would need to be true for this to fail:** Different financial tolerances (one founder needs income sooner than the other), OR different views on strategic direction that cannot be resolved, OR unequal perceived contribution creating resentment.

## Yellow Hat (Positive Thinking): What Could Make This Unstoppable?

**Single hardest-to-replicate asset:** The combination of both founders' regulated financial services experience with experiential delivery capability. Julian's 6 years of brand governance at Amex plus Justin's premium event delivery at Amex creates a pair of credentials that no competitor can replicate — because no other agency was built by two people who both worked inside the same regulated institution's brand and experiential functions.

**Most effective sales tool:** The Tier 1 Compliance Readiness Assessment. It is cheap enough to bypass procurement, fast enough to demonstrate methodology, and specific enough to reveal gaps that naturally lead to Tier 2 engagements. If the diagnostic is excellent, it sells the rest of the business.

**If everything aligns:** If Fisher XP secures a founding client at a bank or insurer within 3 months, delivers a flawless activation with measurable outcomes, and publishes the case study — the business becomes the obvious choice for every regulated brand looking for experiential. The market is wide open. No competitor has claimed this position credibly. The regulatory tailwind (EU AI Act, EAA, DUAA) is real and accelerating. A single excellent case study in financial services experiential could generate 3-5 referral conversations within 6 months.

## Green Hat (Creative): Unconventional Moves

1. **Publish a "UK Experiential Compliance Readiness Index."** Score the top 50 experiential programmes in financial services against the six regulatory frameworks. Publish the methodology and anonymised aggregate findings. Name Fisher XP as the research author. This positions the firm as the authority before it has a single client. Cost: 3-4 weeks of founder time. Value: media coverage, inbound enquiries, thought leadership credibility.

2. **Offer a "Compliance Guarantee" on Tier 2 engagements.** "If any element of this activation fails a compliance review due to a gap in our framework, we will remediate at our cost." This is a bold claim that no competitor will match — because no competitor has a compliance framework to back it. The risk is manageable if the framework is genuinely thorough.

3. **Partner with a compliance consultancy rather than competing.** Approach Bovill, Compliancy Services, or a similar FCA-specialist firm and propose a referral partnership: they refer clients with experiential compliance needs to Fisher XP; Fisher XP refers clients with broader regulatory needs to them. This provides instant credibility through association.

4. **Create a "Compliance-First Creative Brief" template and give it away.** A downloadable template that any brand can use to brief their agency with regulatory requirements built in. Fisher XP's name on every brief. Every download is a lead. Every user becomes familiar with Fisher XP's methodology.

5. **Run a live "compliance audit" on a competitor's public activation.** Attend a major brand activation at a financial services conference, document the compliance gaps (data capture without consent, AI personalisation without disclosure, accessibility failures), and publish the findings as a thought leadership piece. Bold, attention-generating, and demonstrates expertise through action rather than claims.

## Blue Hat (Process): Where Is the Process Broken?

**Dominant process flaw: No sales pipeline infrastructure.** Fisher XP has a detailed service architecture, a pricing model, a verbal identity, and a messaging framework. It does not have: a CRM system configured, a pipeline tracking process, a qualified prospect list, outreach templates, or a follow-up cadence. The business is fully designed and completely unsellable — because the sales process has not been built.

**Most significant mutual blind spot: The founders are both strategists.** Julian is a brand strategist. Justin's most distinctive work is strategic-creative direction. Neither founder is a natural hunter — the person who makes 20 calls a week, follows up relentlessly, and lives in the CRM. The business plan assumes sales will come from thought leadership and warm introductions. This may work eventually, but it is slow. The first 90 days need active outbound selling, and neither founder's CV suggests this is their strength.

**False dichotomy in the strategy: "Compliance OR Creative."** The entire proposition is built on the premise that the market forces buyers to choose between compliance and creativity. What if this is a false dichotomy that exists in agency marketing but not in buyer experience? What if most buyers get compliant-enough creative from their current agency and do not perceive a gap? The research provides evidence of compliance gaps — but from the supply side. No buyer interview has confirmed that this is a felt pain point.

## Conclusion

### Five Significant Concerns

1. **No revenue, no pipeline, no founding client — and the launch plan is still in the planning phase.** The planning-to-action ratio must reverse immediately.
2. **The core assumption (FS CMOs will hire a two-person startup) is completely untested.** Everything depends on this and no amount of additional analysis resolves it.
3. **Cash flow dynamics in experiential marketing are punishing.** The founders need £60K-£80K starting capital and must negotiate milestone-based payment terms on every engagement.
4. **Neither founder is a natural sales hunter.** The first 90 days require aggressive outbound activity that is outside both founders' comfort zones.
5. **Production capability is entirely subcontracted.** One production partner failure on an early engagement could be fatal to the brand.

### Overall Viability Rating: 55%

The proposition is well-researched, well-positioned, and addresses a real gap. But it has zero market validation. The research is strong. The execution has not started. Viability depends entirely on what happens in the first 90 days — specifically, whether the founders can convert their combined networks into a founding client.

### Actions to Increase Viability Above 70%

1. **Identify and approach 3 specific founding client prospects by name this week.** Not "financial services firms" — actual people at actual companies. Richard Thornton at Aviva is the most obvious starting point.
2. **Set up a CRM (HubSpot free tier) and build a 50-prospect list within 5 days.** Every prospect must have a named contact, a known event or experiential programme, and a plausible entry point.
3. **Commission concept portfolio visualisations (£2K-£5K) immediately.** Without visual creative work, every pitch relies on words alone. Buyers want to see what you can do.
4. **Secure Cyber Essentials certification before any financial services approach.** £300-£500, 2-4 weeks. Non-negotiable for the target market.
5. **Agree production partner terms with 3 companies this month.** Rate cards, SLAs, insurance certificates. Without this, you cannot quote on a Tier 2 engagement.
