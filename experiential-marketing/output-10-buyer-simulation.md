# Fisher XP — Buyer Simulation Exercise

Four simulated buyer conversations testing the service architecture against realistic, sceptical buyers. Adapted from Brand Schema Prompt 6.

---

## Buyer 1: Sophie Chen — Head of Events, Barclays Wealth Management

**Profile:** 12 years in financial services events. Reports to CMO. Manages £2.5M annual events budget across 15-20 activations. Currently uses Imagination for flagship events and two boutique agencies for smaller activations. Sophisticated buyer who has seen every agency pitch.

**What triggered her inquiry:** A compliance near-miss at their annual Private Banking Forum — the data capture at registration used AI-powered badge scanning that may fall within EU AI Act scope. Legal flagged it post-event. She's been asked to ensure it doesn't happen again.

### Phase 1: Enquiry

**Sophie:** "I saw your piece on experiential compliance readiness — or someone forwarded it to me, I can't remember. We had a situation at our last investor forum where our registration tech may have crossed an EU AI Act line. Legal are now asking me to audit our entire events programme. Honestly, I don't know where to start. My current agencies just shrug when I ask about compliance — it's not their world."

**What she's really asking:** Can you solve a specific, urgent problem quickly? And are you credible enough for me to bring into a Barclays environment?

### Phase 2: Pitch

**Fisher XP pitches the Experiential Compliance Readiness Assessment (Tier 1, £5K-£8K).**

**Sophie's response:** "The scope makes sense. Ten working days is fine — I need this before our Q3 planning cycle. But I have questions.

First, who actually does the work? I've seen agencies where the senior people pitch and then juniors deliver. With two of you, I assume you're both hands-on, which I like. But what happens if you're both on other projects?

Second, the fee is fine — it's within my discretionary approval limit so I don't need to go through procurement for this. But if the audit leads to a bigger engagement, procurement will be involved, and they'll want to see years of trading history, insurance certificates, and references. Can you pass that gate?

Third, and this is the real question — do you actually know FCA compliance as it applies to events? Not general compliance. Specifically: financial promotions rules at investor events, GDPR as it applies to high-net-worth client data capture, and the new Consumer Duty requirements for client-facing experiences. If you can't speak fluently about those three things, we're wasting each other's time."

### Phase 3: Objections

**Objection 1: "You've never done this for a bank."**
"I can see from your backgrounds that you've both worked at Amex — but Amex is a card company, not a bank. The FCA regulatory environment for wealth management events is different. Our compliance team will want to see that you understand COBS, financial promotions, and how they apply to experiential settings. Can you demonstrate that specifically?"

**Objection 2: "What if the audit recommends changes that cost more than the audit itself?"**
"I've been burned before by consultants who audit and then upsell. If your assessment says we need £50K of remediation work, how do I know that's genuine and not a sales strategy? Do you have a policy on recommending your own services versus recommending the client handles it internally?"

**Objection 3: "I need this to be confidential."**
"This audit will identify compliance gaps in a regulated financial institution. That's sensitive information. What's your data handling policy? Where do you store documents? Do you have Cyber Essentials or ISO 27001? If not, I can't share our event data with you — our IT security team will block it."

## Buyer 2: James Okonkwo — CMO, Fidelity International (UK)

**Profile:** 7 years in role. £15M marketing budget, of which £3M is events and experiential. Uses George P Johnson for global events and a rotating roster of UK boutiques. Frustrated with measurement — the CFO has questioned the experiential budget twice in the last year. Not yet convinced that compliance-integrated experiential is a category.

**What triggered his inquiry:** A board presentation where the CFO asked for ROI evidence on their experiential programme. James could only produce attendance figures and NPS scores. He's looking for a measurement solution.

### Phase 1: Enquiry

**James:** "I'll be honest — I'm not sure I need a 'Regulated Experience House.' What I need is someone who can prove that my events programme is worth £3M a year. My CFO is asking questions I can't answer. I've seen your measurement framework mentioned somewhere — can you walk me through what that actually looks like?"

**What he's really asking:** Solve my CFO problem. The compliance angle is not what drew me in — the measurement angle is.

### Phase 2: Pitch

**Fisher XP pitches the Experiential ROI Measurement Audit (Tier 1, £6K-£10K), leading to Experiential Measurement Framework Build (Tier 2, £20K-£35K).**

**James's response:** "OK, I like the structure — audit first, then build the framework. Two concerns.

One, my events team uses Cvent for registration, Salesforce for CRM, and Tableau for dashboards. Your measurement framework needs to plug into all three. Do you have technical integration capability, or are you going to give me a methodology document and leave me to implement it?

Two, I need the first measurement report in time for our September board meeting. That's 14 weeks away. Can you deliver a working framework — not a proposal for a framework, but actual measurement on an actual event — within that window? We have an adviser conference in July that would be the test case."

### Phase 3: Objections

**Objection 1: "George P Johnson says they can add measurement to their existing contract."**
"I asked GPJ about measurement last month. They offered to add a 'measurement module' to our retainer for £40K/year. It's basically an enhanced wrap report with some attribution modelling. I turned it down because it felt like an upsell on the existing contract. How is yours different — and why should I pay a separate company for something my primary agency says they can do?"

**Objection 2: "Your measurement framework is unproven."**
"You're telling me you have a proprietary methodology. But you don't have a single case study showing it worked for a financial services client. How do I know this isn't a nice theory that falls apart when it meets Salesforce data quality issues and events teams who forget to scan badges?"

**Objection 3: "If I bring in a separate measurement partner, my primary agency will see it as a threat."**
"GPJ won't like it if I hire someone else to measure their work. They'll either feel audited or they'll be uncooperative with data sharing. Have you dealt with this dynamic before? How do you position yourself as complementary rather than adversarial to the lead agency?"

## Buyer 3: Anika Patel — VP Experience, Revolut

**Profile:** 4 years at Revolut, previously at Spotify. Technically sophisticated. Reports to the Chief Marketing Officer. Revolut is expanding into experiential to complement their digital-first brand. Budget: £1.5M for experiential in 2026 (first significant experiential investment). No incumbent agency — building from scratch.

**What triggered her inquiry:** Revolut's CEO wants a physical presence at Money20/20 Europe (Amsterdam, June 2026) that positions Revolut as a serious banking platform, not a fintech app. Anika needs an agency partner fast but doesn't know the experiential landscape.

### Phase 1: Enquiry

**Anika:** "We're doing our first major experiential activation at Money20/20 and I need a partner who gets fintech. Most experiential agencies I've spoken to think 'banking event' means a hotel ballroom with a PowerPoint. I need something that reflects who Revolut actually is — disruptive, tech-forward, bold. But we also have compliance requirements now that we have a banking licence. So it needs to be bold AND compliant. Can you do both?"

**What she's really asking:** Are you creative enough for Revolut's brand, or are you a compliance consultancy in agency clothing?

### Phase 2: Pitch

**Fisher XP pitches a Regulated Experience Design engagement (Tier 2, £60K-£90K for Money20/20 activation).**

**Anika's response:** "I like that you lead with compliance-integrated design rather than bolting it on. But let me push you on the creative side.

Revolut's brand is about speed, simplicity, and challenging conventions. Our marketing is bold — neon colours, sharp copy, irreverent tone. Every agency I've spoken to shows me renders that look like a private bank event. Can you show me creative work that demonstrates you can do bold, disruptive experiences — not just compliant ones?

Also, Money20/20 is in three months. That's a tight timeline for an agency I've never worked with. What's your production plan? Who builds the stand? Who does AV? Who manages on-site in Amsterdam? I need names, not 'we have a network of partners.'"

### Phase 3: Objections

**Objection 1: "Your portfolio doesn't show disruptive work."**
"I've looked at your backgrounds. American Express, Aviva, Alter Domus — those are premium, established brands. I respect that. But Revolut is a different animal. We need an agency that can create something people talk about on Twitter, not something that wins a B2B award. Show me something unexpected in your portfolio."

**Objection 2: "Three months is not enough time to build trust with a new agency."**
"Normally I'd do a smaller project first to test the relationship. But I don't have that luxury — Money20/20 is the brief. What risk mitigation can you offer? A trial period? Performance guarantees? A kill clause if the creative doesn't meet our standards by a specific date?"

**Objection 3: "Your pricing is higher than the other agency I'm considering."**
"I've had a quote from an Amsterdam-based agency that specialises in Money20/20 activations. They're 25% cheaper and they've done six stands at Money20/20 before. What justifies your premium?"

## Buyer 4: Richard Thornton — Group Marketing Director, Aviva

**Profile:** 18 years at Aviva. Oversees a £60M marketing budget. Events budget: £5M across multiple business lines. Uses a roster of five agencies. Has worked with Julian Hayes before on the wealth division repositioning. Knows Brand Schema's work. Sceptical of new agencies but loyal to individuals he trusts.

**What triggered his inquiry:** Julian mentioned Fisher XP in conversation. Richard is intrigued because he trusts Julian's strategic judgement and has an upcoming adviser conference that needs a fresh approach.

### Phase 1: Enquiry

**Richard:** "Julian, you know I respect your work on the wealth repositioning. But you're telling me you've started an events agency? That's a different business. Running a conference for 500 advisers isn't the same as writing a brand strategy. What makes you think you can deliver live events at scale?"

**What he's really asking:** I trust you personally. But can your company deliver? And if it goes wrong, who's accountable?

### Phase 2: Pitch

**Fisher XP pitches a Brand Experience Redesign (Tier 2, £35K-£45K) for the annual adviser conference, with the compliance readiness assessment bundled in.**

**Richard's response:** "The price is fine — it's within my discretionary budget and I can justify it internally. The compliance angle is timely — we had questions from the FCA about our last client event and I'd rather get ahead of it.

But I need to be direct. I'm going to get pushback from procurement. You're a new company. I can argue for you based on the personal relationship, but I need ammunition. What can you give me that makes the procurement case easy? References from Amex? Insurance certificates? A clear scope document that shows this isn't open-ended?"

### Phase 3: Objections

**Objection 1: "My current agency will feel displaced."**
"We use Imagination for our flagship events. If I bring Fisher XP in for the adviser conference, Imagination will see it as losing territory. I don't want to destabilise that relationship. Can you position this as complementary — maybe as a strategic layer on top of their production capability?"

**Objection 2: "What happens when you scale beyond two people?"**
"I like working with senior people directly. That's why I'm interested in Fisher XP. But if this goes well and I want to expand the relationship, what happens? Do I get the same people, or do you hire juniors and I get a watered-down version?"

**Objection 3: "I need this to be seamless with our internal team."**
"Our events team has 8 people. They're experienced operators. They don't need hand-holding on logistics. What they need is strategic direction, creative ambition, and someone to deal with the compliance framework so they don't have to. Can you work embedded with my team rather than as an external agency?"

## Synthesis: What the Simulations Reveal

### Six Gaps in the Service Architecture

1. **Technical integration capability is missing.** James (Fidelity) and Anika (Revolut) both expect integration with CRM, analytics, and event-tech platforms. The service architecture describes measurement methodology but does not address data engineering. Fisher XP needs either a technical partner or a fractional data engineer.

2. **Creative portfolio does not match the positioning.** Anika (Revolut) and Sophie (Barclays) both want to see creative work. The founders' backgrounds demonstrate strategic and brand capability, not experiential creative. Fisher XP needs a visual portfolio — even if it's concept work or redesigns of existing events — before going to market.

3. **Data handling and security credentials are a hard requirement.** Sophie (Barclays) cannot share event data without Cyber Essentials or ISO 27001. This is a procurement gate, not a preference. Fisher XP must invest in Cyber Essentials certification (£300-£500, self-assessment) before approaching financial services clients.

4. **The agency relationship dynamic is underestimated.** James (Fidelity) and Richard (Aviva) both flag that bringing in Fisher XP may threaten the incumbent agency. The service architecture does not address how Fisher XP positions alongside existing agencies — as a strategic layer, a measurement partner, or a replacement.

5. **Production partner specifics are needed for credibility.** Anika (Revolut) wants names, not "a network." Fisher XP must be able to say "Our production is delivered by [Named Partner], who have delivered [X] activations at [Venue/Event]." Vague references to partners destroy credibility with sophisticated buyers.

6. **Sector-specific regulatory knowledge is non-negotiable.** Sophie (Barclays) tested FCA-specific compliance knowledge. Generic "regulated industries" expertise is insufficient. Fisher XP must develop deep fluency in FCA financial promotions, Consumer Duty, and COBS as they apply to experiential — or risk being exposed in the first technical conversation.

### Five Objection Categories with Responses

| Category | Core Objection | Recommended Response |
|----------|---------------|---------------------|
| Track record | "You haven't done this before as Fisher XP." | "Our founders have 20+ combined years in premium brand experiences at Amex, Aviva, and Alter Domus. Fisher XP is new; our expertise is not. We mitigate risk with fixed-scope Tier 1 diagnostics — pay £7K to test our methodology before committing to a larger engagement." |
| Creative credibility | "Show me you can do bold, not just compliant." | "Compliance is the floor, not the ceiling. We design experiences that are remarkable first and compliant by design. Here is a concept [portfolio piece needed] that demonstrates both." |
| Technical capability | "Can your measurement actually plug into our systems?" | "We partner with [Named Technical Partner] for data integration. Our methodology is platform-agnostic; implementation is configured to your stack. We've specified integration with Salesforce, HubSpot, Cvent, and Tableau." |
| Agency politics | "My current agency won't like this." | "We're designed to complement, not replace. We provide the strategic compliance and measurement layer; your lead agency continues to produce. We make their work more defensible and measurable — which protects their relationship with you too." |
| Procurement gates | "You don't meet our supplier requirements." | "We hold £[X]M PI insurance and Cyber Essentials certification. For the first engagement, we recommend a Tier 1 diagnostic within your discretionary approval limit. This builds the track record needed for procurement approval on larger engagements." |

### Three Changes to Service Architecture

1. **Add a "Strategic Layer" engagement type** — specifically designed for clients who have an incumbent production agency but want Fisher XP for strategy, compliance, and measurement. Fee: £15K-£30K per activation. This addresses the agency politics objection and opens a larger market than full-service delivery.

2. **Bundle Cyber Essentials certification into launch prerequisites** — it is cheap (£300-£500), fast (2-4 weeks for self-assessment), and removes a hard procurement gate. Without it, financial services is inaccessible.

3. **Create a visual portfolio before going to market** — commission concept work for 3-4 hypothetical activations (investor day, adviser conference, Money20/20 stand, product launch) that demonstrate creative ambition alongside compliance integration. Budget: £2K-£5K for freelance visualisations. Without this, every creative credibility objection is unanswerable.
