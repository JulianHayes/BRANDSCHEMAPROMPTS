# Fisher XP — Checkpoint Review

Gap analysis conducted after reviewing all four research outputs and the Red Team critique.

---

## What Is Missing From This Body of Research That Will Be Obvious in Hindsight

### 1. The 25% Disenchanted Spend Figure Is Built on Proxies, Not Direct Evidence

The market sizing report is transparent about this: "No single published figure exists for the percentage of experiential spend that is 'disenchanted.'" The 25% base case is triangulated from three data points that measure different things:

- 48% of ISBA members not getting value for money (measures satisfaction, not switching intent)
- 39% of CMOs planning agency cuts (measures intent across all agency types, not experiential specifically)
- 70% unable to prove experiential ROI (measures measurement frustration, not willingness to change provider)

The leap from "dissatisfied" to "will switch to a new entrant" is enormous. Most dissatisfied clients renegotiate terms, reduce scope, or in-house — they do not hire a solo operator they have never heard of. A more defensible disenchanted figure for a new entrant's addressable market would be 5-8% of the total — brands actively looking for alternatives AND willing to take a risk on an unproven provider. That turns the £525M base case into £105M-£168M. Still substantial, but a different conversation.

**Action required:** Before using the £525M figure in any pitch or business plan, validate with 5-10 buyer interviews. Ask: "If you were dissatisfied with your current experiential agency, what would you actually do?" The answer will calibrate the real switching rate.

### 2. The Competitive Landscape Has a Temporal Blind Spot

The competitive intelligence scores 30 giants and 12 boutiques as they exist today. It does not model what happens in 12 months if the opportunity is real.

Specific gaps:

- **No assessment of holding company response speed.** If compliance-integrated experiential is genuinely demanded, WPP's VMLY&R, Publicis Events, or dentsu's MBS could launch a "Regulated Experience" practice within 6 months by hiring 2-3 specialists. The research scores them on "Legacy Drag" but does not model their ability to fast-follow.

- **No assessment of management consultancy entry.** Accenture Song, Deloitte Digital, and EY already operate in adjacent spaces. They have regulatory credibility that an experiential startup cannot match. If they see the compliance-experiential intersection, they will enter from the compliance side — which is harder to defend against than entry from the creative side.

- **No emerging AI-native competitors identified.** The research acknowledges this gap but does not fill it. Somewhere, another founder is running similar analysis and reaching similar conclusions. The first-mover window may be shorter than 12-18 months.

### 3. "The Regulated Experience House" May Be Defensible But Not Buyable

The Red Team flagged this and the panel partially addressed it, but the core tension remains unresolved:

- **Elena and Marcus agree** that compliance-first positioning is defensible
- **Priya notes** that no CMO wakes up thinking "I need a compliance-integrated experiential agency"
- **The research confirms** that CMOs buy impact, memorability, and brand differentiation

The positioning needs a front door and a back door. The front door is what makes a CMO take the meeting: "We create premium brand experiences that survive regulated environments without creative compromise." The back door is what makes procurement approve: compliance integration, measurement framework, transparent pricing.

Currently the research and panel lean toward leading with the back door (compliance). The Red Team correctly identifies this as a risk. The Checkpoint recommendation: **lead with creative ambition, close with compliance credibility.**

### 4. The Revenue Model Assumes Bespoke Services, but the Red Team Recommends Productised Diagnostics

The panel recommends Year 1 revenue of £250K-£400K from 3-5 clients at £50K-£100K. The Red Team recommends starting with a productised diagnostic at £8K.

These are fundamentally different business models:

- **Bespoke at £50K-£100K:** Long sales cycles (3-6 months), high-touch delivery, founder-dependent, 2-3 clients maximum in Year 1 realistically
- **Productised at £8K:** Shorter sales cycles, repeatable delivery, scalable, but requires 30-50 clients to reach £250K

The research does not model the hybrid path: use the £8K diagnostic to generate pipeline for £50K-£100K engagements. This is the most likely viable path, but the conversion rate from diagnostic to strategic engagement is unknown and untested. The 60-70% conversion assumption from the Brand Schema work does not transfer — different market, different buyer, different price point.

**Action required:** Model three revenue scenarios: (a) pure bespoke, (b) pure productised, (c) hybrid with explicit conversion rate assumptions. Test conversion assumptions in the first 3 engagements.

### 5. Cash Flow Gap Between Winning and Getting Paid Is Not Modelled

The market sizing models revenue, not cash. For experiential marketing specifically:

- Typical payment terms for enterprise clients: 60-90 days from invoice
- Invoice timing: often upon completion, not upon signing
- Project duration: 4-12 weeks
- Total cash gap: potentially 4-6 months from winning a brief to receiving payment

For a £80K engagement won in Month 3:
- Month 3-5: Delivery (founder time + subcontractor costs paid upfront)
- Month 5: Invoice issued upon completion
- Month 7-8: Payment received

Meanwhile, subcontractors expect payment within 30 days. The founder is funding the gap personally.

**Action required:** Build a cash flow model (not a revenue model) showing monthly cash position for the first 18 months. Include: insurance (£8K-£15K upfront), subcontractor payments (30-day terms), client payments (60-90 day terms), tools and overhead. Identify the maximum cash gap and how it will be funded.

### 6. The Panel May Be Confirming the Research Rather Than Challenging It

The strategic panel (Elena, Marcus, Priya) was given three research documents and asked to recommend positioning. Their recommendation aligns closely with White Space 2 (Compliance-Integrated Experiential Delivery) identified in the demand-side analysis.

This is logical — but it means the panel is selecting from options the research defined, not discovering options the research missed. Potential blind spots:

- **The panel did not consider whether experiential marketing is the right vehicle at all.** Perhaps the founder's skills are better deployed in a different format (consultancy, platform, content production).
- **The panel did not stress-test the founder's actual desire to run events.** Experiential marketing involves physical logistics, on-site management, weather contingencies, health and safety responsibility, and weekend work. The research assumes the founder wants this. Does he?
- **The panel did not consider partnership or acquisition as alternatives to solo launch.** Joining an existing boutique as a partner, or being acquired into a mid-size agency, might de-risk the path while providing immediate infrastructure.

### 7. The Biggest Untested Assumption

**That financial services CMOs will hire a solo operator for high-stakes brand experiences in a regulated environment.**

The entire proposition depends on this. The research provides evidence that:
- The demand exists (compliance gaps, measurement gaps, creative fatigue)
- The supply is inadequate (giants too rigid, boutiques under-resourced)
- The market is large enough (£147M base case in UK financial services)

But it does not provide evidence that:
- A CMO at a bank will sign a £50K-£100K contract with a one-person company
- Procurement at a regulated firm will approve a vendor with zero years trading
- The reputational risk of hiring an unproven agency for a high-visibility activation is acceptable

The Red Team identified this. The panel addressed it partially (Priya's procurement barriers, Marcus's sub-£25K entry strategy). But no amount of research resolves this — only actual buyer conversations will.

**The single most important next step is not more research. It is 10 conversations with actual buyers in financial services experiential marketing. Everything else is theory until those conversations happen.**
