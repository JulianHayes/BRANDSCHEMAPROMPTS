# **Contents**

# **Executive Summary**

**Money is pouring into experiential — but it is looking for things that do not exist yet. **UK event budgets recorded their strongest sustained growth in a decade (IPA Bellwether net balance +16.6% for 2025/26), and 74% of Fortune 1000 marketers plan to increase experiential spend. Yet the supply side is structured around a delivery model that cannot serve what buyers now want.

The core finding is not that experiential is dying. It is that a category bifurcation is underway: traditional formats (trade shows, one-off activations, standard exhibition stands) are contracting in real terms, whilst immersive, data-integrated, compliance-ready, creator-embedded experiences are growing at 8–31% CAGR depending on definition. The gap between these two trajectories is the white space.

This report identifies eight white spaces where genuine demand exists and current supply — from both global giants and UK boutiques — is structurally inadequate. It covers budget migration patterns, six overlapping regulatory drivers, audience shift implications, and collision zone analysis across accessibility, AI personalisation, creator economy integration, sustainability, and neuroinclusive design.

# **2. Budget Migration Map**

## **The headline: experiential is growing, but structurally uneven**

The overall experiential marketing services market sits at approximately $52–54 billion globally (2024), growing at roughly 3–5% CAGR. The immersive sub-segment, however, is growing at 31.4% CAGR (Grand View Research). This differential is the story.

## **Where money is flowing FROM**

**Traditional exhibitions are not recovering. **The CEIR Total Index reached 95.6 in Q4 2024 — still 4.4% below pre-pandemic levels. Only 34.1% of individual exhibitions have surpassed their 2019 performance. Attendance lags worst: down 17.8% from 2019 as of Q3 2024. Real revenues (inflation-adjusted) trail by 15.5–16.3%. European exhibitor space has declined at -1.4% per annum since 2019 (UFI).

**Agency allocations are being cut. **Gartner’s 2025 CMO Spend Survey found 39% of CMOs plan to reduce agency allocations. Average agency share of marketing budget fell to 20.7%, continuing a five-year erosion. Among B2B firms, 15.2% have already cut agency spend specifically due to AI (Marketing Week). Overall marketing budgets have flatlined at 7.7% of revenue for a second consecutive year.

**Main media advertising is contracting in the UK. **The IPA Bellwether Q1 2025 recorded the first overall UK marketing budget decline in four years (net balance -4.8%). Video spend declined in Q4 2024 (net balance -10.7%).

## **Where money is flowing TO**

**Events in the UK: the standout category. **IPA Bellwether data shows events have seen consistent budget increases every quarter since 2022. Q1 2024 recorded a series-record expansion at net balance +23.1%. For the 2025/26 financial year, events had the highest anticipated growth of any marketing category at +16.6%.

**Immersive and technology-enhanced formats. **Digital experiential solutions are growing at 8.2% CAGR through 2032, roughly double the overall experiential market. Events and experiential marketing within immersive marketing specifically is forecast at 31.4% CAGR to 2030.

**Experiential retail and pop-ups. **Pop-up retail is projected at $95 billion in revenue in 2025. ICSC projects experiential retail growing from $132 billion (2025) to $543 billion by 2035. Experiential programming generated 34% foot traffic increases in measured retail locations.

**Permanent experiential venues. **Netflix House opened its first permanent experiential location in November 2025. Disney’s $60 billion decade-long experiential pipeline and Universal’s $7 billion Epic Universe illustrate the capital investment race.

**In-house capabilities. **90% of UK marketers are either using or considering in-housing (IPA). Pinterest maintains a robust in-house team for experiential concept development, engaging specialist agencies only for production.

## **The core answer**

Money is not leaving experiential. It is leaving traditional, episodic, agency-dependent experiential and moving towards formats that are: permanent or semi-permanent rather than one-off; data-integrated rather than measurement-dark; content-generating rather than content-consuming; revenue-contributing rather than pure cost centres; and blended across retail, entertainment, and marketing rather than sitting neatly in a single category.

# **3. Collision Zone Analysis**

## **Accessibility regulation + live experiences — Emerging**

The European Accessibility Act became enforceable on 28 June 2025, requiring event websites, apps, registration platforms, ticketing systems, and self-service kiosks to meet WCAG 2.2 Level AA standards. Fines vary by member state: Germany imposes €10,000–€100,000; Italy €5,000–€40,000; France €1,500–€3,000 per offence.

The disability economy represents a $23 trillion global market opportunity, yet 72–75% of leading online brands in Ireland and Germany failed accessibility audits before the EAA deadline (Bird & Bird). The Cannes Lions 2025 Grand Prix for Brand Experience went to “Caption with Intention” — an accessibility-first design system.

Best positioned: specialist accessibility consultancies and event-tech platforms with built-in compliance. Worst positioned: creative experiential agencies that treat accessibility as a retrofit.

## **AI personalisation + physical events — Bifurcated maturity**

For conferences and B2B events, AI concierge tools, session recommenders, and networking matchmakers are now deployed at scale. 50% of meeting planners planned to use AI technology in 2025 (Amex GBT). For brand experiential activations (pop-ups, product launches, festivals), AI personalisation remains nascent. Netflix’s “Mood Studio” at Cannes Lions 2025 represents the frontier, not the norm. Training staff (30%) and cost (25%) remain the top obstacles.

Best positioned: event-tech companies (Cvent, Eventify) and large integrated agencies with data capability. Worst positioned: smaller creative agencies without technology integration.

## **Wellness and sensory design + brand experiences — Genuine demand**

An estimated 15–20% of the global population is neurodivergent. Google partnered with FIRST Agency to produce The Neu Project — a comprehensive neuroinclusive event design guide. KultureCity has certified over 350 sensory-inclusive venues across four countries. The global sensory rooms market was estimated at $1.21 billion in 2024, growing at 10.7% CAGR through 2031.

Best positioned: specialist inclusive design consultancies. Worst positioned: high-energy creative agencies whose default mode is sensory maximalism.

## **Creator economy + experiential — Established and accelerating**

The global creator economy is projected to grow from $191 billion (2025) to $528 billion by 2030 at 22.5% CAGR. StubHub data showed influencers, podcasters, and authors sold 500% more event tickets in 2025 versus 2024. Unilever’s CEO announced increasing influencer spend from 30% to 50% of the marketing budget. The brief is shifting from booking talent to co-designing the experience with creators as structural partners.

Best positioned: creator-native agencies and hybrid experiential/social agencies. Worst positioned: traditional experiential agencies that view creators as “talent to book.”

## **Sustainability reporting + events — Indirect but tightening**

CSRD mandates Scope 3 disclosure, which covers event procurement. Scope 3 typically represents 70–90% of a company’s carbon footprint. ISO 20121 was updated in 2024. However, 76% of experiential marketers lack a formal sustainability policy. The demand is present in conference procurement but largely absent from brand activation briefs.

Best positioned: sustainability consultancies with event expertise. Worst positioned: agencies without carbon measurement capability.

## **AI-mediated discovery + brand experience — Nascent but consequential**

Gartner projected traditional search volume would drop 25% by 2026. Nearly 60% of searches now end without a website visit. Zoom’s CMO: “A brand has to drive connection to the customers, and AI is accelerating the need for experiential touch points.” Experiential becomes the source material that feeds AI systems’ understanding of a brand, not merely a downstream activation channel.

Best positioned: integrated agencies connecting experiential to content strategy and AI visibility. Worst positioned: agencies measuring success solely through on-site footfall.

# **4. Audience Shift Implications**

## **Attention has fragmented — but live experiences command premium pricing**

Screen-based attention has fallen from approximately 2.5 minutes per task (2004) to 47 seconds (2024/25). Yet McKinsey’s 2025 analysis reveals live experiences command the highest monetisation rates: $33/hour for live sport, $24/hour for amusement parks, $17/hour for live concerts — versus $0.25/hour for social media. 48% of attendees reported not feeling actively involved during events (Bizzabo 2024). Agencies must shift from designing dwell-time installations to designing micro-interaction architectures.

## **Trust has pivoted from “we” to “me”**

The 2025 Edelman Trust Barometer documents a fundamental pivot. 80% of people trust brands they use, placing “My Brands” above business, media, government, and NGOs. But the basis of trust has shifted: consumers want economic hope, personal stability, community, and education — not performative societal gestures. For experiential, this means designing for tangible personal value rather than abstract cause-alignment.

## **Co-creation is now a baseline expectation**

87% of Gen Z are more likely to trust brands that invite direct participation (Freeman 2025). Event Marketer’s TrendWatch 2025 identified a preference for “low-key vibes” and “micro events” — smaller-scale experiences where audience members shape outcomes. The agency role shifts from producing spectacle to designing frameworks for participation.

## **Digital-physical integration is moving beyond QR codes**

The phygital solutions market was valued at $25 billion in 2023, projected to reach $52.5 billion by 2030 at 11.2% CAGR. QR codes remain widely adopted (47% of event professionals use them), but the trajectory is towards NFC wristbands, facial recognition, and embedded digital layers that operate without conscious user action. The brief is shifting from “add a digital element” to “design a system where digital and physical are indistinguishable.”

## **Audiences expect post-event community**

Forrester predicts that by 2026, a third of consumers will opt for offline brand experiences, but simultaneously expects brands to build ongoing engagement pipelines around those moments. 48% of thriving Gen Z communities now incorporate in-person elements alongside digital engagement. The deliverable is no longer “the event” but “the community that the event catalyses.”

# **5. Regulatory Demand Drivers**

## **UK Data (Use and Access) Act 2025**

Received Royal Assent on 19 June 2025. Relaxed cookie consent for analytics and functionality, and direct marketing codified as a “legitimate interest.” However, PECR fines now align with UK GDPR maximums at £17.5 million or 4% of worldwide turnover, up from £500,000. Cookie rules now extend to any party who “instigates” cookie placement — creating potential liability for experiential agencies configuring tracking on client event microsites.

## **EU AI Act**

Prohibited practices (subliminal manipulation, real-time remote biometric identification) have been in force since 2 February 2025. This directly constrains facial recognition and emotion-detection technologies at events. The critical date is 2 August 2026, when high-risk AI system requirements become fully applicable. Event personalisation engines, AI networking matchmakers, and profiling tools may fall within scope. Penalties reach €35 million or 7% of global turnover. Over 50% of organisations say they are unclear on requirements.

## **European Accessibility Act**

Enforceable since 28 June 2025. Requires event websites, apps, registration platforms, ticketing, and self-service kiosks to meet WCAG 2.2 / EN 301 549 standards. Extends beyond digital: AV production must ensure live captioning, audio description, and accessible interfaces. 72–75% of leading online brands failed accessibility audits before the deadline. UK businesses selling into EU markets must comply.

## **CSRD and Scope 3**

CSRD mandates Scope 1, 2, and 3 GHG disclosure. Scope 3 includes business travel, venue services, catering, and event procurement — 70–90% of a typical company’s carbon footprint. Post-Omnibus I simplification, scope narrowed to companies with >1,000 employees and >€450m net turnover. CSRD-subject clients will increasingly require carbon data from event suppliers as a tender condition.

## **UK DMCCA and Green Claims Code**

The Digital Markets, Competition and Consumers Act took full effect on 6 April 2025. The CMA can now impose fines of up to 10% of global turnover without court proceedings for misleading sustainability claims. Any sustainability claims made at or about experiential events must be substantiated.

## **UK Online Safety Act**

Event platforms enabling attendee messaging, community walls, social feeds, or Q&A features are in scope. Phase 1 (illegal harms) enforceable since 17 March 2025; Phase 2 (child safety) since October 2025. Penalties reach £18 million or 10% of qualifying global revenue. No evidence exists of experiential agencies engaging with OSA requirements.

## **Combined regulatory burden**

These six regulatory domains — data protection, AI, accessibility, sustainability, consumer protection, and online safety — create overlapping obligations. A single brand activation with an event app, AI-driven personalisation, sustainability claims, and creator-generated content potentially falls within scope of every regulation listed above. No experiential agency has published a compliance readiness framework addressing this combined landscape.

# **6. White Space Synthesis**

The following eight white spaces are identified based solely on the evidence gathered above. Each represents a zone where demand is documented but current supply — from both the global giants and UK boutiques — is structurally inadequate.

### **White space 1: Experiential measurement-as-a-service**

**Demand evidence. **70% of event organisers reported difficulty proving ROI (Bizzabo). Audible’s CFO described a measurement category called “feels right” because experiential is too difficult to measure rigorously. The industry lacks a standardised metric equivalent to digital GRPs.

**Why current suppliers are poorly positioned. **Giants are creative and production companies, not measurement methodologists. Their incentive structures favour selling activations, not proving they worked. Boutiques lack data engineering and statistical capability entirely.

**Addressable opportunity. **[UNCONFIRMED] If experiential spend is ~$54bn globally and 70% of organisers cannot prove ROI, a standardised measurement platform capturing 1–2% of spend implies $500m–$1bn addressable market.

**UK verticals most affected: **B2B (trade shows), finance (compliance-driven ROI evidence), tech (data-literate CMOs demanding attribution).

### **White space 2: Compliance-integrated experiential delivery**

**Demand evidence. **EU AI Act (Aug 2026), EAA (in force), UK DUAA (£17.5m PECR fines), CSRD Scope 3, UK Online Safety Act collectively create a regulatory surface touching every aspect of modern experiential activation. 50%+ unclear on AI Act; 72–75% failed EAA audits; 76% lack sustainability policy.

**Why current suppliers are poorly positioned. **Giants outsource compliance to client legal teams. Boutiques score low on compliance readiness and governance maturity. No agency has published a combined regulatory readiness framework.

**Addressable opportunity. **[UNCONFIRMED] Fee premium for compliance-ready delivery estimated at 15–25% above standard rates, based on analogous premiums in regulated industries.

**UK verticals most affected: **Finance (FCA-regulated), pharma (MHRA advertising codes), luxury (EU-facing trade).

### **White space 3: Neuroinclusive and low-sensory experience design**

**Demand evidence. **15–20% of the global population is neurodivergent. EAA mandates accessibility. Disability-inclusive campaigns deliver 65% higher ROI and 18% higher engagement. Google commissioned The Neu Project. KultureCity has certified 350+ venues. Sensory rooms market $1.21bn, growing 10.7% CAGR.

**Why current suppliers are poorly positioned. **Giants operate in a creative culture that rewards sensory maximalism. Neuroinclusive design requires restraint, quiet, choice — and occupational therapy expertise outside standard agency skill sets. Boutiques lack operational depth for regulatory compliance.

**Addressable opportunity. **[UNCONFIRMED] Conservative estimate of neuroinclusive design services within events: $300–600m globally, extrapolated from sensory rooms market and neurodivergent population share.

**UK verticals most affected: **Public sector (Equality Act), luxury (personalised experiences), finance, health and wellness brands.

### **White space 4: Event-to-community pipeline design**

**Demand evidence. **Forrester predicts 1/3 of consumers will opt for offline experiences by 2026. Edelman names “community” as a key brand role. Only 30% of first-time attendees return. 15% of organisers rate networking “very effective.”

**Why current suppliers are poorly positioned. **Experiential agencies sell projects, not platforms. Revenue model (ideation → production → execution → wrap report) is structurally opposed to ongoing community management. Boutiques lack technology infrastructure.

**Addressable opportunity. **[UNCONFIRMED] Community platform market projected $1.2bn by 2027. Experiential-to-community pipeline: potentially $200–400m in design and management fees.

**UK verticals most affected: **Tech (community-led growth), B2B (membership organisations), DTC/consumer brands (loyalty).

### **White space 5: Creator-embedded experience design**

**Demand evidence. **Creator economy projected $528bn by 2030. 41% of US social media users attended an in-person influencer event. StubHub showed 500% growth in creator-driven ticket sales. Unilever shifting from 30% to 50% influencer spend. Pinterest co-designed Cannes activation with creators from concept stage.

**Why current suppliers are poorly positioned. **Experiential agencies and influencer agencies operate as separate disciplines. Giants can bolt on influencer partnerships but rarely co-design. Boutiques lack social media measurement and creator relationship infrastructure.

**Addressable opportunity. **[UNCONFIRMED] IAB estimates US creator economy ad spend at $37bn; experiential integrations at 5–10% implies $1.8–3.7bn in US alone.

**UK verticals most affected: **Consumer brands, luxury, food and beverage, fashion, entertainment.

### **White space 6: Physical experience design for digital-native brands**

**Demand evidence. **Discord, DoorDash, and Canva made their physical debut at Cannes Lions 2025. Netflix invested in permanent experiential venues. 77% of Gen Z and Millennials have planned travel around visiting a branded location. Experiential retail projected $132bn to $543bn (2025–2035).

**Why current suppliers are poorly positioned. **Giants are geared towards FMCG, automotive, and luxury clients with established physical operations. Translating digital brand identities into physical form requires fundamentally different capability. Boutiques lack technology integration and scale that digital-native brands expect.

**Addressable opportunity. **[UNCONFIRMED] Pop-up retail projected $95bn in 2025. Digital-native subset is smaller but rapidly growing; no direct sizing available.

**UK verticals most affected: **Tech, fintech, e-commerce, gaming, streaming.

### **White space 7: Real-time experiential data integration (CRM pipeline)**

**Demand evidence. **Brand marketers describe weeks-long lag between event data capture and CRM integration. Multiple automotive brands pulled out of auto shows citing inability to prove ROI. Customer Data Platforms are the fastest-growing martech category at 35% CAGR.

**Why current suppliers are poorly positioned. **This is a data engineering problem, not a creative one. Giants are creative and production houses. Boutiques lack technical depth entirely. The integration challenge spans event-tech, CRM, marketing automation, and analytics pipelines.

**Addressable opportunity. **[UNCONFIRMED] CDP market projected $28.2bn by 2030 (Grand View Research). Experiential integration subset is a fraction, but value to brands is disproportionate.

**UK verticals most affected: **B2B (lead-to-pipeline attribution), automotive, finance, tech.

### **White space 8: Experiential as AI-era brand proof point**

**Demand evidence. **Gartner projects 25% decline in traditional search by 2026. 60% of searches end without website visits. Zoom CMO and Duolingo creative director explicitly link increased experiential investment to AI-driven brand discovery challenges. 52% report reduced engagement when they suspect unlabelled AI authorship.

**Why current suppliers are poorly positioned. **Giants measure experiential success through footfall, social impressions, and media value — metrics disconnected from AI visibility. Boutiques lack strategic planning capability to position experiential within AI-era discovery architecture.

**Addressable opportunity. **[UNCONFIRMED] Emerging strategic service, not yet a defined market. Premium for connecting experiential to AI-era brand strategy is currently unquantifiable.

**UK verticals most affected: **DTC brands, tech, luxury (heritage storytelling), finance (trust-dependent).
