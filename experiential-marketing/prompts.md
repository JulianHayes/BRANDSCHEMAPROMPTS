## Red Team

Run before Prompt 1. New conversation.

| Setting | Value |
|---|---|
| Conversation | New conversation (solo prompt) |
| Web Search | OFF |
| Output | 1500-2000 word critique |
| Time | 30 minutes |

### Context

Fisher XP is a proposed AI-native experiential marketing business founded by Justin Fisher.
Thesis: the experiential marketing industry is structurally unable to serve premium brands in regulated industries — compliance, measurement, and creative rigour are all inadequate.
Target sectors: financial services, B2B technology, luxury.
The founder has completed initial research (competitive landscape, demand-side white space, market sizing, and a strategic panel review) and is about to build the business.

### Prompt

Before building further, act as a critical advisor.

Tell me:
1. What are the 10 things most founders forget to address when entering the experiential marketing industry as a solo operator?
2. What would the top 1% of experiential agency founders do that this founder is unlikely to think of?
3. What questions should the founder be able to answer before going to market that he probably cannot answer yet?
4. Looking at the stated positioning ("The Regulated Experience House" for premium brands in regulated industries), what are the most dangerous blind spots?
5. What financial, operational, or market timing risks is this venture missing?
6. The research identified 8 white spaces. Which are genuinely defensible for a new entrant and which are traps that will attract larger competitors within 12 months?
7. The strategic panel recommended targeting financial services (28%), B2B tech (25%), luxury (22%). Is this spread too wide for a single founder? What would you cut?

Do not be polite. Be useful. Flag anything that would change direction if the founder knew about it now.

## Prompt 1: Competitive Landscape

Run first in the research sequence. New conversation with web search ON.

| Setting | Value |
|---|---|
| Conversation | New conversation |
| Web Search | ON |
| Output | Structured tables and analysis |
| Time | 90 minutes |

### Prompt

You are a Senior Marketing Analyst conducting competitive intelligence on the experiential marketing industry. Use British English. Write with precision. No hype or filler.

## YOUR ROLE AND CONSTRAINTS

You are building an evidence pack, not writing strategy. Your job is to find, organise and cite what exists. Where evidence does not exist, say so clearly.

Hard evidence rules (apply to every claim in this document):
- Every numeric claim must have a citation directly next to it.
- If you cannot confirm a claim, write: [UNCONFIRMED] then provide the closest defensible proxy or range, with citations explaining why you chose it.
- Do not include any agency unless you can cite evidence it is materially active in experiential marketing (not just advertising, PR, or digital).
- No invented companies, numbers, or quotes.
- Where a citation comes from your training data rather than a live search, flag it as [TRAINING DATA - VERIFY INDEPENDENTLY].
- Prefer industry reports from Freeman, EventMB/Skift, WARC, Statista, Campaign, Event Marketer, or company filings over news articles.

## STEP 1: DEFINE SCOPE

Before listing any agencies, define 'experiential marketing' for this report.

Include: live brand experiences, immersive events, experiential activations, pop-ups, trade show design and activation, brand land/permanent experiences, launch events with participatory elements, experiential retail environments.

Exclude: pure media buying, traditional advertising, PR-only, social media management without a live component, conference/congress logistics-only (AV, catering, registration), pure digital marketing, influencer marketing without live activation.

Then define operational criteria you will apply consistently:
- 'Giant': annual revenue above GBP 20M (or equivalent), 200+ employees, multinational footprint or dominant single-market position, broad service offering including strategy through to production.
- 'Boutique': annual revenue below GBP 10M, under 50 employees, primarily UK-based, specialist or niche positioning.

## STEP 2: BUILD THE CANDIDATE UNIVERSE

Identify the Top 30 relevant players across global and UK experiential marketing. Then identify 10-15 UK boutique players.

Starting names to VALIDATE (not copy blindly): Jack Morton, Momentum, George P Johnson, Amplify, Imagination, TRO, Wasserman. For boutiques: Backlash, iMP, Swerve, Pearl Lemon.

For each candidate, search for evidence of experiential marketing activity. Remove any that do not fit. Add others that do.

## STEP 3: SCORE USING THE RUBRIC BELOW

Apply these scoring definitions exactly. Do not create your own rubric.

### GIANTS: 'Legacy Drag' Score (0-5)

0 = No evidence of this drag factor.
1 = Minor signs but actively mitigated.
2 = Present but manageable; some client impact.
3 = Clearly visible; appearing in client feedback, trade press, or pitch losses.
4 = Significant; structurally embedded and affecting competitiveness.
5 = Severe; a defining characteristic that drives client churn.

Score each giant on these six dimensions and average for the overall score:
a) Overhead intensity: ratio of fixed costs to project delivery costs.
b) Delivery model rigidity: reliance on fixed teams vs flexible/freelance models.
c) AI adoption in measurement and operations: use of AI for attribution, personalisation, or operational efficiency.
d) Creative repeatability: evidence of formulaic or repetitive creative output across clients.
e) Procurement friction: evidence of mark-up opacity, slow contracting, or complex commercial structures.
f) Client concentration risk: dependency on a small number of large accounts.

### BOUTIQUES: 'Resource Ceiling' Score (0-5)

0 = No evidence of this limitation.
1 = Minor constraint, actively managed.
2 = Present but workable for current scale.
3 = Clearly limiting growth or capability.
4 = Significant barrier to competing for mid-market and above.
5 = Severe; prevents credible competition beyond small briefs.

Score each boutique on these five dimensions and average for the overall score:
a) Scalability: ability to deliver multi-market or high-volume programmes.
b) Compliance readiness: preparedness for 2026 UK and EU data rules affecting event data capture.
c) Operational depth: resilience to handle concurrent complex projects.
d) Talent bandwidth: access to senior strategic and creative talent beyond founders.
e) Governance and reporting maturity: ability to meet enterprise procurement requirements for reporting, insurance, and audit.

## OUTPUTS REQUIRED

Deliver in this exact order:

1. Scope definitions (as written above, confirmed or amended with reasoning).

2. Table A: Top 30 Giants. Columns: Name | HQ | UK presence (yes/no with evidence) | What they sell (1 line) | Typical client types (1 line) | Strengths (2 bullets) | Legacy drag score (0-5, averaged) | Evidence for legacy drag (2 bullets per dimension scored 3+) | Citations.

3. Table B: UK Boutiques (10-15). Columns: Name | Base city | What they sell (1 line) | Sweet spot (1 line) | Constraints (2 bullets) | Resource ceiling score (0-5, averaged) | Evidence for ceiling (2 bullets per dimension scored 3+) | Citations.

4. Claims register: list every numeric claim, its citation, and a confidence note (confirmed / training data / unconfirmed with proxy).

Output tables as clearly formatted text tables. Stop after delivering these outputs. Do not write strategy, positioning, or recommendations.

## Prompt 2: Demand-Side White Space

Run after Prompt 1. New conversation with web search ON.

| Setting | Value |
|---|---|
| Conversation | New conversation |
| Web Search | ON |
| Output | Structured analysis with tables |
| Time | 120 minutes |

### Prompt

You are a Market Research Analyst investigating where genuine demand exists in experiential marketing that no supplier is currently serving well. Use British English. Write with precision. No hype or filler.

## YOUR ROLE AND CONSTRAINTS

You are looking for DEMAND-SIDE white space, not supplier weaknesses. The distinction matters:
- Supplier weakness = 'Agency X is bad at measurement.' That is a competitive gap, not white space.
- Demand-side white space = 'Brands are writing briefs for [something] and getting no credible responses, or the need is so new that briefs do not exist yet.'

Your job is to find evidence of emerging demand patterns that existing agencies are not equipped to serve. Use web search extensively.

Apply the same hard evidence rules as before:
- Every numeric claim must have a citation next to it.
- If you cannot confirm a claim, write: [UNCONFIRMED] then provide a proxy with citations.
- Flag training data citations as [TRAINING DATA - VERIFY INDEPENDENTLY].

## INVESTIGATION 1: WHAT ARE BRANDS TRYING TO BUY THAT THEY CANNOT FIND?

Search for evidence across these channels:
- RFP trends and procurement briefs (search marketing procurement platforms, industry bodies like ISBA, ANA, WFA).
- Recurring conference themes at Event Marketer, Cannes Lions Experience track, SXSW, C2 Montreal, Campaign Experience Conference.
- Industry surveys on unmet client needs (Freeman, EventMB/Skift, Bizzabo, Splash, Cvent annual reports).
- LinkedIn and trade press commentary from senior brand-side marketers expressing frustration with what is available.

For each demand signal you find, document:
a) What the demand is, in plain language.
b) Who is expressing it (role, sector, geography if known).
c) Why existing suppliers appear unable to serve it.
d) The citation.

Do not limit yourself to a predetermined list of white spaces. Follow the evidence.

## INVESTIGATION 2: WHERE ARE BUDGETS MIGRATING?

Search for evidence of budget movement patterns:
- What are CMOs and heads of experience pulling money OUT of? (Which formats, which agency types, which delivery models?)
- What are they putting money INTO? (New formats, in-house capabilities, technology, adjacent categories?)
- Is experiential as a category growing or contracting? At what rate? In which sub-segments?

Key question to answer: Is money leaving experiential entirely, or is it leaving TRADITIONAL experiential and looking for something that does not have a name yet? These are fundamentally different dynamics.

For each migration pattern, document:
a) Direction of budget flow (from X to Y).
b) Scale if available (percentage shift, absolute figures).
c) Reason cited by the source.
d) The citation.

## INVESTIGATION 3: WHAT COLLISIONS ARE CREATING NEW DEMAND?

Search for evidence of adjacent markets colliding with experiential in ways that create new categories of demand. Investigate these collision zones (and any others you discover):

- Accessibility and inclusion regulation + live experiences: Are new legal requirements (EAA 2025, UK Accessibility Action Plan) creating demand for experience design capabilities that experiential agencies do not currently have?
- AI personalisation + physical events: Is demand emerging for real-time, data-driven personalisation within live experiences? What does that look like? Who is asking for it?
- Wellness and sensory design + brand experiences: Is there genuine demand for low-sensory, 'zen-generation' experience design, or is this just trend-piece noise? What is the evidence?
- Creator economy + experiential: Are brands looking for agencies that can blend live activation with creator-driven content in ways that go beyond 'invite influencers to the event'?
- Sustainability reporting + events: Are procurement teams adding sustainability measurement requirements to experiential briefs? What standards are they referencing?
- AI-mediated discovery + brand experience: As consumers increasingly discover brands through AI assistants and recommendation engines, is this changing what brands need from live experiences? Is experiential becoming more important (as the 'proof point' AI cannot replicate) or less?

For each collision zone with genuine evidence of demand:
a) Describe the emerging need.
b) Estimate maturity: nascent (conference chatter only), emerging (appearing in briefs), established (agencies are being hired for this).
c) Note which existing agency types are best and worst positioned.
d) The citation.

## INVESTIGATION 4: WHAT DO THE BUYERS' BUYERS NEED?

Your prompt's brands are selling to end consumers and B2B audiences. Search for evidence of shifts in AUDIENCE expectation that create upstream demand for new experiential capabilities:

- Attention and engagement: How are audience attention spans and engagement patterns changing at live events? What does recent research say?
- Trust and authenticity: Is audience scepticism towards branded experiences increasing? What do they want instead?
- Participation vs observation: Is there evidence that audiences expect to co-create experiences rather than passively consume them? What does this mean for design?
- Digital-physical integration: What do audiences expect in terms of seamless digital and physical experience? Are QR codes and event apps still working, or is demand shifting?
- Post-event value: Is there evidence that audiences expect ongoing value or community from a brand experience, rather than a one-off moment?

For each audience shift with evidence:
a) Describe the shift.
b) Note the implications for what brands will need to buy.
c) The citation.

## INVESTIGATION 5: REGULATORY AND COMPLIANCE DEMAND DRIVERS

Search specifically for regulatory changes that will create new demand in experiential marketing by 2026:
- UK Data Protection and Digital Information Act implications for event data capture.
- EU AI Act requirements affecting personalisation at events.
- European Accessibility Act (June 2025) requirements for experiential environments.
- Sustainability disclosure rules (CSRD, UK equivalents) affecting event procurement.
- Any other regulatory developments you find that affect experiential.

For each regulation:
a) What it requires, specifically, in the context of experiential marketing.
b) When it takes effect.
c) Whether existing agencies appear prepared (search for evidence).
d) The citation.

## OUTPUTS REQUIRED

Deliver in this exact order:

1. Demand signals register: Every demand signal found, in a table. Columns: Signal description | Source type (RFP/survey/trade press/conference/regulation) | Sector | Geography | Maturity (nascent/emerging/established) | Citation.

2. Budget migration map: A clear summary of where money is moving, with evidence.

3. Collision zone analysis: Each zone with evidence of genuine demand, rated by maturity.

4. Audience shift implications: What end-audience changes mean for upstream demand.

5. Regulatory demand drivers: Each regulation and its specific experiential marketing implications.

6. White space synthesis: Based ONLY on the evidence gathered above, identify the 5-8 most compelling white spaces where genuine demand exists and current supply is inadequate. For each white space:
a) Name it clearly.
b) Summarise the demand evidence (with citation references).
c) Explain why current suppliers (giants and boutiques) are poorly positioned to serve it.
d) Estimate addressable opportunity if any sizing data exists, or mark [UNCONFIRMED] with a reasoning note.
e) Note which UK verticals (luxury, finance, B2B, tech) are most affected.

7. Claims register: Every numeric claim, its citation, and a confidence note.

Stop here. Do not write positioning, offers, or strategy recommendations. This is evidence only.

## Prompt 3: Market Sizing and Opportunity Quantification

Run after Prompt 2. New conversation with web search ON.

| Setting | Value |

| Conversation | New conversation |
| Web Search | ON |

| Time | 90 minutes |

### Prompt

You are a Financial Analyst building market sizing and scenario models for the experiential marketing industry. Use British English. Show all working.

## YOUR ROLE AND CONSTRAINTS

You are quantifying the opportunity identified in prior research. Your job is to find the best available market data, be transparent about its limitations, and build defensible scenarios.

Apply the same hard evidence rules:
- Every numeric claim must have a citation next to it.
- If you cannot confirm a claim, write: [UNCONFIRMED] then provide a proxy with citations.
- Flag training data citations as [TRAINING DATA - VERIFY INDEPENDENTLY].
- When sources conflict, show all figures and explain why they differ (definition scope, geography, methodology).

## STEP 1: MARKET SIZE RANGES FOR 2026

Search for and compile the best available estimates for:
a) Global experiential marketing market size for 2025 and 2026 (or nearest available years). Include source, year of publication, definition used, and any noted methodology.
b) UK experiential marketing market size for 2025 and 2026 (or nearest). If a UK-specific figure is not available, provide a derivation method (e.g. UK share of global, UK share of European, bottom-up from IPA Bellwether or AA/WARC data) and show your working.

Present as a range table showing low, mid, and high estimates with the source for each.

## STEP 2: 'DISENCHANTED SPEND' EVIDENCE

Search for evidence that experiential marketing spend is 'at risk' due to any of these factors:
- Client trust issues with agencies (cite surveys, trade press, procurement reports).
- Measurement dissatisfaction (cite specific studies on experiential ROI measurement gaps).
- Governance and compliance concerns.
- Procurement friction (mark-up opacity, slow contracting).
- Poor experience quality or creative fatigue.

Compile what you find. If you cannot find a clean 'at risk' percentage specifically for experiential, say so, then propose a defensible range with citations justifying your reasoning. You may draw on adjacent data (e.g. general agency satisfaction surveys, marketing budget reallocation data) but label these clearly as proxies.

## STEP 3: SCENARIO MATHS

Build three scenarios for the percentage of total experiential spend that is 'disenchanted' (at risk of switching provider or being reallocated):

- Low case: 15% (conservative assumption).
- Base case: Use a cited figure if you found one. If not, pick a defensible midpoint and explain why.
- High case: 35% (aggressive assumption).

For each scenario, calculate and show line-by-line working:
a) Global disenchanted value (USD) = Global market size x disenchanted percentage.
b) UK disenchanted value (GBP) = UK market size x disenchanted percentage.
c) UK priority verticals breakdown: estimate the share attributable to luxury, finance, B2B, and tech. Use sector marketing spend data to justify the split. Show your working.

## STEP 4: WHY BRANDS LEAVE (EVIDENCE LIST)

Create two tables:

Table 1: Why brands leave giants. 6 drivers minimum. Columns: Driver | Evidence/citation | Measurable procurement requirement this maps to (e.g. 'Agency must demonstrate open-book pricing with maximum 15% management fee').

Table 2: Why brands leave boutiques. 6 drivers minimum. Same columns.

Each driver must have a citation or be marked [UNCONFIRMED] with a proxy.

## OUTPUTS REQUIRED

Deliver in this exact order:

1. Market size range table: Global and UK, with source comparison and explanation of differences.
2. At-risk spend evidence summary.
3. Scenario maths: Three scenarios shown line by line with all working visible.
4. Churn drivers: Two tables (giants and boutiques) tied to procurement requirements.
5. Claims register: Every numeric claim, its citation, and a confidence note.

Stop here. Do not write positioning, offers, or strategy recommendations. This is evidence only.

## Checkpoint

Run after reviewing all four research outputs. Same conversation as Prompt 4 (or standalone).

| Setting | Value |
|---|---|
| Conversation | Same conversation as Prompt 3 output review, or new |
| Web Search | OFF |
| Output | 800-1200 word gap analysis |
| Time | 60 minutes |

### Context

You have completed four research outputs for Fisher XP:
1. Competitive Landscape (30 giants, 12 boutiques, scored)
2. Demand-Side White Space (8 white spaces, demand signals, collision zones)
3. Market Sizing (global and UK sizing, disenchanted spend, scenario models)
4. Strategic Panel Review (Elena, Marcus, Priya — converged on "The Regulated Experience House")

Before proceeding with service design, messaging, or go-to-market planning, conduct a gap review.

### Prompt

Tell me what is missing from this body of research that will be obvious in hindsight.

Focus on:
1. Unvalidated assumptions being treated as facts — particularly the 25% disenchanted spend figure and the £250K-£400K Year 1 revenue target
2. Whether the competitive landscape has blind spots — are there emerging AI-native experiential competitors not captured?
3. Whether "The Regulated Experience House" positioning is genuinely defensible or whether any well-resourced agency could claim it within 6 months
4. Whether the target verticals (financial services 28%, B2B tech 25%, luxury 22%) are the right entry point for a solo founder
5. Cash flow realities — the research models revenue but does not model the gap between winning work and getting paid
6. Whether the panel's recommendation is too influenced by the research it was given, rather than independent market testing

Do not ask for clarification. Make your best assessment based on what has been produced.

## Prompt 4: Strategic Panel Review

Run after Prompts 1-3. New conversation with all research outputs provided as context.

| Setting | Value |
|---|---|
| Conversation | New conversation with all prior outputs attached |
| Web Search | OFF |
| Output | Comprehensive panel assessment and converged recommendation |
| Time | 120 minutes |

### Prompt

## SETUP: THE PANEL

You will role-play three distinct expert advisors. Each has a sharply different lens. They will first work independently, then challenge each other, then converge on a shared recommendation.

The three panellists:

ELENA (Brand Strategist): A world-renowned brand strategist with 25 years of experience building and repositioning premium and luxury brands. She has led positioning work for brands including fashion houses, private banks, and luxury hospitality groups. She thinks in terms of distinctiveness, emotional resonance, cultural relevance, and long-term brand equity. She is allergic to generic positioning and will push hard for a point of view that only this company could credibly own. She is sceptical of technology-led positioning unless it serves a genuine human need.

MARCUS (Tech Entrepreneur and VC): A successful founder who built and exited two B2B SaaS companies before moving into venture capital, where he now backs AI-native businesses. He evaluates opportunities through the lens of defensibility, scalability, unit economics, and speed to revenue. He is excited by AI-native business models but ruthlessly practical about what 'AI-native' actually means operationally versus what sounds good in a pitch deck. He will challenge any positioning that cannot be tied to a monetisable capability.

PRIYA (CMO, Premium Brand): CMO of a FTSE 250 company that operates at the premium end of its market. She has a GBP 40M+ marketing budget and buys experiential marketing services regularly. She represents the buyer's perspective. She knows what it is like to sit through agency pitches, manage procurement requirements, and justify experiential spend to a CFO. She will test every recommendation against the question: 'Would I actually buy this, and could I get it through my procurement process?'

## CONTEXT DOCUMENTS

You have been provided with the following documents. Read all of them before any panellist speaks.

Research outputs (from prior prompts):
- Document 1: Competitive Landscape - maps the top 30 experiential marketing giants and 10-15 UK boutiques, scored for legacy drag and resource ceiling respectively.
- Document 2: Demand-Side White Space - identifies genuine demand signals, budget migration patterns, market collision zones, audience shifts, and regulatory drivers creating unmet demand.
- Document 3: Market Sizing and Opportunity Quantification - sizes the global and UK experiential market, models disenchanted spend scenarios, and maps churn drivers.

Founder materials:
- CV: Professional background of the founder.
- Portfolio: Recent work examples demonstrating the founder's capabilities.
- Questionnaire answers: The founder's initial thinking on the business, captured in response to a structured questionnaire.

## THE FOUNDER'S VISION (for context, not as a constraint)

The founder envisions an AI-native experiential marketing business. His analogy: AI forms the skeleton of the company (the structural framework that everything hangs on), creativity is its flesh (the visible, tangible output that clients and audiences experience), and insights are its central nervous system (the intelligence that connects everything and drives decision-making).

The panel should take this vision seriously but is free to challenge, refine, or redirect it based on the evidence.

## PHASE 1: INDEPENDENT ASSESSMENTS

Each panellist reviews all documents independently and provides their assessment. No panellist should reference another's views in this phase.

### ELENA's brief (Brand Strategist):

Review the founder's CV, portfolio, and questionnaire answers alongside the competitive landscape research. Answer these questions:

a) What is genuinely distinctive about this founder's background and capabilities? Not 'what is good' but 'what could only he credibly claim'? Be specific and cite evidence from the CV and portfolio.

b) Looking at the white space research, which 2-3 white spaces are the best fit for this specific founder's strengths? Not the biggest opportunities in abstract, but the ones where his particular background creates an unfair advantage. Explain your reasoning.

c) Propose 2-3 distinct positioning territories for the business. For each one:
   - A working positioning statement (one sentence: For [target], [company] is the [frame of reference] that [point of difference] because [reason to believe]).
   - Why this positioning is defensible (what stops a giant or boutique from copying it?).
   - What this positioning requires the business to say NO to (every good positioning excludes something).
   - A candid risk assessment: what could go wrong with this positioning?

d) What is your honest reaction to the AI-as-skeleton analogy? Is it a genuine strategic differentiator or a hygiene factor that every agency will claim within 18 months?

### MARCUS's brief (Tech Entrepreneur / VC):

Review all documents with a focus on commercial viability. Answer these questions:

a) Based on the market sizing and disenchanted spend analysis, what is a realistic revenue target for year 1 and year 3? Show your reasoning. Do not be optimistic for the sake of it.

b) What does 'AI-native' actually mean for an experiential marketing business in operational terms? Specifically:
   - Which functions should be AI-powered from day one (not aspirationally, but practically)?
   - Where does AI create genuine cost advantage or capability advantage versus competitors?
   - Where is 'AI-native' just marketing language with no defensible moat?

c) Looking at Elena's positioning territories (once she has proposed them), which is most fundable and why? Which has the clearest path to revenue?

d) What is the biggest commercial risk you see in this venture, based on the evidence? What would you want to see de-risked in the first 6 months?

e) React to the founder's questionnaire answers: where is the thinking sharp and where is it vague or wishful?

### PRIYA's brief (CMO / Buyer):

Review all documents from the perspective of someone who would actually hire this company. Answer these questions:

a) Based on the founder's CV and portfolio, would you take a meeting with this company? Be honest. What would make you say yes, and what would give you pause?

b) Looking at the churn drivers from the market research (why brands leave giants and boutiques), which 3 drivers does this new business have the most credible answer to? Which would you not believe from a startup?

c) What would this company need to demonstrate in a pitch to win your business over an established agency? Be specific about proof points, case studies, guarantees, or commercial structures.

d) React to each of Elena's positioning territories from a buyer's perspective: which one would actually make you pick up the phone? Which sounds like every other agency pitch you have heard?

e) What procurement barriers would a business like this face selling to companies like yours? Be specific (insurance minimums, track record requirements, payment terms, data handling certifications, etc.).

## PHASE 2: CHALLENGE ROUND

Now the three panellists can see each other's Phase 1 responses. Each panellist must:

a) Identify the single most important point raised by each of the other two panellists that changes or sharpens their own thinking.

b) Challenge one specific claim or recommendation from each of the other two panellists. Be constructive but direct. Do not agree for the sake of politeness.

c) State whether their own initial assessment has changed, and how.

Keep this phase concise. No more than 300 words per panellist.

## PHASE 3: CONVERGED RECOMMENDATION

The panel now works together to deliver a single, unified recommendation. Where they disagree, they should note the disagreement honestly rather than papering over it.

### Output 1: Recommended Brand Positioning

Deliver ONE recommended positioning, structured as:
- Positioning statement (one sentence, using the For/Is/That/Because format).
- The 3 words that should define the brand's personality and tone.
- What this company must be known for (the one thing).
- What this company must never be (the anti-positioning).
- The 'only we' test: complete the sentence 'We are the only experiential company that...' If you cannot complete it credibly, the positioning is not distinctive enough. Go back and sharpen it.

Also provide 1-2 alternative positionings that the panel considered strong but did not recommend as the primary. Explain why they were set aside and under what circumstances the founder might revisit them.

### Output 2: Value Proposition

Deliver a value proposition structured for three audiences:

a) For the CMO or Head of Experience (the person who wants the work): What do they get that they cannot get elsewhere? How does it make them look good internally?

b) For Procurement (the person who needs to approve the spend): What commercial model, governance, and risk mitigation makes this an easy yes? Reference specific procurement pain points from the research.

c) For the CFO (the person who challenges the budget): What measurable business outcome does this deliver? How is ROI demonstrated? Be specific, not vague.

### Output 3: Strategic Line of Sight

Map the recommended positioning onto the white spaces identified in the research. Deliver this as a clear roadmap:

a) PRIMARY WHITE SPACE (attack first, 0-12 months): Which specific white space should the business enter first? Why this one? What does the minimum viable service look like? What are the first 3 clients or client types to pursue?

b) SECONDARY WHITE SPACES (expand into, 12-24 months): Which 1-2 additional white spaces become accessible once the primary position is established? What capability or proof point from the primary space unlocks them?

c) HORIZON PLAYS (24-36 months): Based on the market trajectory from the research, where could this business be positioned in 3 years that would be very difficult for competitors to follow? This is where the AI-native skeleton should create genuine structural advantage.

d) WHAT TO IGNORE: Which white spaces from the research, however attractive they look, are wrong for this founder and this business? The panel must name at least 2 and explain why.

### Output 4: Honest risks and prerequisites

The panel must agree on:
- The 3 biggest risks to this venture succeeding, based on everything reviewed.
- The 3 things the founder must have in place before going to market (non-negotiable prerequisites).
- The 1 thing the panel most disagrees about, stated openly so the founder can make his own judgement.

## FORMAT AND TONE

- Use British English throughout.
- Write with precision and restraint. No hype, no filler, no agency-speak.
- Each panellist should have a distinct voice. Elena is incisive and culturally literate. Marcus is direct and commercially blunt. Priya is pragmatic and slightly sceptical.
- When referencing the research documents, be specific (e.g., 'the demand signal register identified...' or 'Table A shows that...'). Do not make vague gestures at 'the research'.
- The total output should be substantial but not padded. Quality over quantity.

## CRITICAL INSTRUCTION

Do not flatter the founder. Do not tell him his CV is impressive unless that assessment is backed by specific evidence of why it creates competitive advantage. Do not tell him his questionnaire answers are insightful unless they genuinely are. The most valuable thing this panel can do is be honest. A polite panel is a useless panel.

### Context

You have completed the strategic panel review (Prompt 4). The panel converged on "The Regulated Experience House" positioning with specific risks and prerequisites identified.

Now build a decision framework: what specific conditions would cause the founder to stop, pivot, or continue?

### Prompt

Build a kill criteria framework for Fisher XP.

For each criterion, specify:
1. The measurable condition (specific, not vague)
2. The decision date (by when must this be true?)
3. What happens if it fails (pivot to subcontracting? Reduce scope to single vertical? Shut down?)
4. How you will know it has failed (specific evidence)

Generate five criteria specific to Fisher XP and the experiential marketing context. Examples of the format (do not use these):
- "If no signed client engagement above £30K by end of Month 4, pivot to subcontracting for established agencies while building pipeline"
- "If the first two client engagements do not generate measurable compliance or measurement outcomes, abandon 'Regulated Experience House' positioning and revert to generalist experiential"

After the five criteria, answer:
1. Which criterion would actually cause the founder to pivot, and which would he push on even if it failed?
2. What is the single most dangerous assumption underpinning the entire venture that has not yet been tested with real buyers?
3. At what monthly burn rate does the founder need to make a go/no-go decision, and by when?
