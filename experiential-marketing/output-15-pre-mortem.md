# Fisher XP — Pre-Mortem and Consolidated Recommendations

Imagine Fisher XP has failed by March 2027. What happened? Adapted from Brand Schema Prompt 9b.

---

## Failure Narrative 1: Market Failure (By Month 8)

Fisher XP launched in Q2 2026 with strong positioning, comprehensive messaging, and a detailed service architecture. The founders attended three financial services conferences, published four thought leadership pieces, and had 12 introductory conversations with potential buyers.

None converted.

The problem was not awareness — people knew Fisher XP existed. The problem was timing and buying behaviour. Financial services firms plan experiential budgets 6-12 months in advance. By the time Fisher XP launched, the 2026 budget was allocated. The compliance readiness diagnostic generated interest but not urgency — "interesting, but we'll look at this for 2027 planning." The measurement framework was compelling in conversation but unproven in practice, and no buyer was willing to be the first test case.

By Month 8, the founders had spent £58K (including setup costs) and invoiced £4K from a single diagnostic at a mid-tier insurance broker. The pipeline showed three "interested but not yet" conversations. Both founders began freelancing to cover personal costs, reducing Fisher XP to two days per week. By Month 10, the venture was effectively dormant.

**Root cause:** The founders launched into a budget cycle that was already committed. They needed to sell in Q1 2026 for Q3-Q4 2026 delivery — six months before the business formally existed.

---

## Failure Narrative 2: Execution Failure (By Month 12)

Fisher XP secured a founding client — an adviser conference for a mid-size wealth manager, won through Julian's Aviva network. Fee: £38K. The founders were elated.

The engagement went wrong in three ways. First, scope creep: the client added a compliance audit for their entire events programme to the brief, without adjusting the fee. The founders, desperate to prove themselves, absorbed the extra work. Second, production: the AV subcontractor underdelivered at the venue, producing a technical failure during the keynote session. Fisher XP had selected the cheapest quote rather than the most reliable partner. Third, measurement: the attribution framework was designed but not implemented in time. The post-event report was delivered on day 18, not day 10 as promised.

The client was polite but did not renew. The case study was unusable — the founders could not reference a project with a visible production failure. Internally, the experience created tension: Julian felt the scope creep should have been managed more firmly; Justin felt the production partner should have been vetted more thoroughly. Both were right. Neither issue was resolved.

By Month 12, Fisher XP had completed one engagement, made a net loss of £4K on it (after absorbing scope creep), and had no referenceable case study. The second client was still in negotiation. Both founders were freelancing 60% of the time.

**Root cause:** First-engagement desperation led to poor boundaries (scope), poor procurement (cheapest partner), and over-promising (10-day attribution on an untested system).

---

## Failure Narrative 3: Competitive Failure (By Month 14)

In September 2026, Dentsu announced "dentsu Regulated Experience" — a new practice combining Merkle's data capability with MBS's event production, led by a former FCA communications director. The announcement included three launch clients: Barclays, AXA, and Legal & General.

Fisher XP's positioning — "The Regulated Experience House" — was neutralised overnight. Every prospect conversation shifted: "We've seen Dentsu's announcement. They're offering something similar with a global team and established client relationships. What do you offer that they don't?"

The founders struggled to answer. Fisher XP's differentiation was compliance-first creative and designed-in measurement. Dentsu could claim both, plus 500 employees, 30 years of case studies, and existing procurement approval at every target account.

By Month 14, Fisher XP had three completed engagements (total revenue £62K) but pipeline had dried up. The two prospects who had been in active conversation paused to evaluate Dentsu's offering. The founders considered pivoting to a niche that Dentsu would not pursue — accessible experience design or sustainability measurement — but neither aligned with their core expertise.

**Root cause:** The positioning occupied a space that a well-resourced competitor could claim credibly within 6 months. Fisher XP did not build enough client relationships or proprietary methodology to create a moat before the window closed.

## Consolidated Recommendations

### Part A: Five Critical Risks with Mitigations

| Risk | Trigger Event | Mitigation |
|------|--------------|------------|
| Budget cycle mismatch — launching too late for 2026 spend | Fewer than 3 budget-holding conversations by end of Month 2 | Begin selling NOW, before formal launch. Position Tier 1 diagnostics as "pre-budget input" for 2027 planning. Get into 2026 budgets through existing agency relationships (subcontracting or strategic layer). |
| First engagement failure | Production issue, scope creep, or missed deliverable on first project | Vet production partners on reliability, not cost. Define scope with iron-clad change order process. Under-promise on measurement timing (15 days, not 10) until the system is tested. |
| Cash exhaustion before meaningful revenue | Cash reserves below 3 months of burn before Month 6 | Both founders maintain freelance capability as active fallback. Negotiate 30/30/40 payment terms on every Tier 2 engagement. Never accept 60-day post-completion terms on the first 3 projects. |
| Giant fast-follow neutralises positioning | A holding company or consultancy announces a "regulated experiential" practice | Build client relationships that are personal, not institutional. Create proprietary methodology that is named, documented, and perceived as unique. Publish the Compliance Readiness Index before a competitor can. Speed is the only defence. |
| Co-founder misalignment | Disagreement on pricing, client prioritisation, or financial tolerance that is not resolved within 48 hours | Establish a formal decision-making protocol before launch: who has final say on what? Define individual financial red lines (minimum draw, maximum personal investment). Schedule monthly co-founder reviews with a structured agenda. |

### Part B: Five Untested Assumptions with Cheap Validation

| Assumption | Why It Matters | Cheap Validation Method |
|-----------|----------------|----------------------|
| FS CMOs will hire a two-person startup | The entire proposition depends on this | 10 buyer conversations. Ask: "Would you meet with a two-person specialist firm for a £7K diagnostic?" Track yes/no ratio. Need >50% yes. |
| Compliance integration commands a price premium | Without a premium, margins collapse on Tier 2 | Present two identical proposals to 5 prospects: one with compliance integration at £80K, one without at £60K. Track which gets more engagement. |
| The measurement framework works in practice | "Attribution within 10" is a core commitment | Build a prototype measurement framework and test it on a pro bono or reduced-fee engagement. If the data pipeline breaks, better to discover now than on a paying client. |
| Tier 1 diagnostics convert to Tier 2 at 30-40% | Revenue model depends on this funnel | Track the first 10 diagnostics rigorously. If conversion is below 20%, the funnel does not work and pricing or scope must change. |
| Production partners will deliver to Fisher XP's standard | Reputation depends on subcontractor quality | Run a paid test project (even internal) with each production partner before using them on a client engagement. Evaluate reliability, communication, and quality. |

### Part C: Service Architecture Changes

Based on the Six Hats analysis and failure narratives:

1. **Add a "Strategic Layer" engagement as a formal Tier 2 service** — the buyer simulation revealed that many clients will keep their incumbent agency and want Fisher XP for compliance and measurement only. This is a faster, lower-risk entry point than full activation delivery.

2. **Reduce the 10-day attribution commitment to 15 working days** until the measurement framework has been tested on 2+ real engagements. Over-promising on an untested system is the highest-probability execution failure.

3. **Create an explicit "Founding Client" package** — reduced fee (30-40% discount), enhanced senior access, co-creation of the case study, and a 12-month retainer lock-in. This acknowledges the reality: the first client is taking a risk, and they should be rewarded for it.

4. **Do not offer full activation delivery (Regulated Experience Design) until the second or third engagement.** The first 1-2 engagements should be diagnostics, compliance strategies, or measurement frameworks — services that can be delivered by the founders alone without production partner risk.

### Part D: Messaging Changes

1. **Lead with measurement, not compliance, in outreach.** The buyer simulations showed that James (Fidelity) was drawn by measurement, not compliance. Sophie (Barclays) was drawn by a specific compliance incident. Measurement is a more universal pain point. Adjust the outreach sequence: measurement hook → compliance credibility → creative ambition.

2. **Drop "The Regulated Experience House" from outreach subject lines and opening messages.** It is a positioning descriptor, not a sales hook. Use it in proposals and brand materials, not in cold outreach. Cold outreach should lead with a specific problem statement: "Can you prove your events programme is worth £3M?" not "We are the Regulated Experience House."

3. **Add a "What We Don't Do" section to proposals.** The verbal identity guide emphasises accountability. Being explicit about what Fisher XP does not do (large-scale production, logistics-only, pure digital) builds credibility and distinguishes from agencies that claim everything.

### Part E: Kill Criteria Reminder

The three most important kill criteria from the Kill Criteria framework:

1. **First signed engagement worth £15K+ by end of Month 5.** Without this, the business model is unvalidated.
2. **Cash reserves never below 3 months of burn.** The only criterion that forces a decision regardless of optimism.
3. **Named, documented measurement methodology with one proof point by Month 6.** Without this, the core differentiator is a claim, not a capability.

### Part F: Confidence Score

**Overall viability: 55%**

Same as the Six Hats assessment. The proposition is sound. The execution has not started. Viability is entirely dependent on what happens in the next 90 days.

**What would need to be true to raise viability above 70%:**

1. A founding client signed by end of Month 3 (raises to 60%)
2. First engagement delivered successfully with a referenceable outcome (raises to 65%)
3. Measurement framework tested and producing real attribution data (raises to 70%)
4. Second client secured through referral or inbound, not personal network (raises to 75% — this proves the market, not just the relationship)

Each of these is sequential. There are no shortcuts. The founders cannot reach 70% viability through additional planning, research, or documentation. Only client engagements move the number.
